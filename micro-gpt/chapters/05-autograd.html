Automatic Differentiation

The Value class is the engine of learning. It is a "smart number" that remembers how it was built — so the computer can figure out, on its own, how to make the model better.

Where we are We have numbers (vectors), and we know how to turn scores into probabilities (softmax). But how does the model learn? Learning means nudging thousands of numbers in the right direction. To know which direction is "right", we need derivatives. This chapter is the most important one in the whole course — take your time.

1. What is a derivative?

Forget every calculus class you may have slept through. A derivative answers one plain question:

The only definition you need A derivative is a rate of change: "If I nudge the input by a tiny bit, how much does the output change, and in which direction?"

That's it. A derivative is a number. A big derivative means the output is very sensitive — a small push on the input causes a big change in the output. A small derivative means the output barely reacts. A negative derivative means pushing the input up makes the output go down.

But why do we need derivatives at all? Because training means adjusting thousands of numbers, and for each one we have to know which way to push it — up or down — to make the model less wrong. Without derivatives we'd be blind: our only option would be to try a random nudge, re-run the entire model, and check whether the loss dropped. With 4,192 parameters that's thousands of blind guesses per training step. A derivative hands us the exact direction directly — and, as we'll see, one backward pass computes all 4,192 of them at once.

The speed analogy

Speed is the most familiar derivative in the world. Suppose you drive and after 2 hours you have covered 100 km. Your average speed is:

speed = change in position / change in time = 100 km / 2 h = 50 km/h

Speed is "the derivative of position with respect to time": if time moves forward a tiny bit, how much does your position change? 50 km for every hour. Position is the output, time is the input, and speed is the rate of change between them. You have been computing derivatives your whole life without calling them that.

The tangent-line picture

Draw any curve. Pick a point on it. Now lay a straight ruler against the curve so it just kisses that one point — that line is the tangent. How steep the tangent is (its slope) is the derivative at that point.

  • Curve going steeply up → large positive derivative.
  • Curve going steeply down → large negative derivative.
  • Curve momentarily flat (top of a hill, bottom of a valley) → derivative is zero.

Demo: slope of the tangent = the derivative

The blue curve is a simple hill. The orange line is the tangent at the red point. Slide the point: where the hill rises, the slope (derivative) is positive; at the very top it is zero; on the way down it is negative.

The good news We will never compute a derivative by hand. We do not need calculus formulas. The Value class computes every derivative for us, automatically. That is literally what "automatic differentiation" (autograd) means. Our only job is to understand what the number means, not how to derive it on paper.

2. The chain rule — the one idea that makes it work

There is exactly one calculus concept you must absorb, because the entire engine is built on it: the chain rule. It tells you how to find the rate of change through a chain of steps.

The salary analogy

Imagine a chain of three things that depend on each other:

  • Doubling your study hours doubles your grade. (rate = ×2)
  • Doubling your grade doubles your salary. (rate = ×2)

So what happens to your salary if you double your study hours? You don't add the effects — you multiply them:

study → salary  =  (study → grade) × (grade → salary)  =  2 × 2 = 4×

Double the study hours, quadruple the salary. The rates of change multiply along the chain. That is the chain rule, in plain words.

The formula (don't panic)

If some final value f depends on h, and h depends on x, then:

df/dx  =  df/dh  ×  dh/dx

Read df/dx as "how much f changes when x changes". The formula says: to get the rate from x all the way to f, multiply the rate of each link in the chain. The "study → grade → salary" example is this formula with all rates equal to 2.

Why this matters for a neural network A neural network is a giant chain of tiny operations: multiply, add, multiply, add... thousands of links. The final output is a single number called the loss (how wrong the model is). To improve, we need the rate of change of the loss with respect to every parameter. The chain rule lets us compute all of them by multiplying local rates backward through the chain. This backward sweep is called backpropagation.
Real-life analogy Backpropagation is tracing back a food chain. A restaurant dish tastes bad, so the chef traces backward: "Was it the sauce? → Was it the tomatoes in the sauce? → Was it the supplier of those tomatoes?" Each step asks "how much did this ingredient contribute to the bad taste?" Backpropagation does exactly that: it traces back through every computation to find how much each parameter contributed to the error.

3. The Value class — a Java autograd engine

Here is the heart of microGPT. Every number that flows through the model is not a plain double — it is a Value. A Value is a smart number that remembers how it was computed, so it can later trace the chain rule backward.

// Autograd engine for recursive chain rule application
// through a computation graph
record Value(double[] data, double[] grad,
             Value[] children, double[] localGrads) {

    Value(double v) {
        this(new double[]{v}, new double[]{0},
             new Value[0], new double[0]);
    }

    static Value of(double v) { return new Value(v); }

    double d() { return data[0]; }          // the actual number
    double g() { return grad[0]; }          // the gradient
    void addGrad(double v) { grad[0] += v; } // accumulate gradient
}

Four fields, and each one has a clear job:

FieldPlain-English meaning
dataThe actual number. If this Value holds 6.0, then data[0] is 6.0. (It's a 1-element array only so the value can be mutated in place — a Java trick, nothing deep.)
gradThe gradient: how much the final loss changes if this number changes. Starts at 0. This is the derivative we care about. The whole point of the engine is to fill this in.
childrenWhich Values were used to build this one. If c = a + b, then c's children are a and b. This is the "memory" of how it was computed.
localGradsThe local derivative of this operation with respect to each child — one link of the chain. For c = a + b, both local grads are 1.
data vs. grad — keep them separate in your head data = "what is the number?" (flows forward, left to right). grad = "how sensitive is the loss to this number?" (flows backward, right to left). Forward fills data; backward fills grad. Two passes, two fields.

4. Every operation, with real numbers

Each math operation is a method on Value. It does two things at once: computes the forward result (the new data) and records the local gradients (the chain-rule links). Let's walk every single one with concrete numbers, using a = 3 and b = 2.

add — addition

Value add(Value o) {
    return op(d() + o.d(),
              new Value[]{this, o},
              new double[]{1, 1});   // dc/da = 1, dc/db = 1
}

If c = a + b, then nudging a up by a tiny amount pushes c up by exactly the same amount — so the rate is 1. Same for b. Both contribute equally.

c = 3 + 2 = 5   |   dc/da = 1,   dc/db = 1

mul — multiplication

Value mul(Value o) {
    return op(d() * o.d(),
              new Value[]{this, o},
              new double[]{o.d(), d()});  // dc/da = b, dc/db = a
}

This one is sneaky and beautiful. If c = a × b, then the rate of change of c with respect to a is b — because each extra unit of a adds b to the product. And the rate with respect to b is a. The derivatives are crossed over.

c = 3 × 2 = 6   |   dc/da = b = 2,   dc/db = a = 3

pow — raising to a power

Value pow(double e) {
    return op(Math.pow(d(), e),
              new Value[]{this},
              new double[]{e * Math.pow(d(), e - 1)});  // n·a^(n-1)
}

The "power rule". If c = an, the rate is n × an-1. With a = 3, n = 2:

c = 3² = 9   |   dc/da = 2 × 3¹ = 6

exp — the exponential

Value exp() {
    double e = Math.exp(d());
    return op(e, new Value[]{this}, new double[]{e});  // dc/da = e^a
}

The exponential ea has a magical property: it is its own derivative. The rate of change equals the value itself. With a = 3:

c = e³ ≈ 20.09   |   dc/da = e³ ≈ 20.09

log — the natural logarithm

Value log() {
    return op(Math.log(d()),
              new Value[]{this},
              new double[]{1 / d()});   // dc/da = 1/a
}

The derivative of log(a) is simply 1/a. With a = 3:

c = log(3) ≈ 1.099   |   dc/da = 1/3 ≈ 0.333

relu — the gate

Value relu() {
    return op(Math.max(0, d()),
              new Value[]{this},
              new double[]{d() > 0 ? 1 : 0});  // 1 if positive, else 0
}

ReLU (we meet it properly in the next chapter) keeps positive numbers and zeroes out negatives. Its derivative is a switch: if the input was positive, the rate is 1 (it passed straight through); if it was negative, the rate is 0 (the gate was shut, so nudging the input changes nothing).

relu(3) = 3, dc/da = 1   |   relu(−3) = 0, dc/da = 0
Notice the pattern Every operation records just its own local link of the chain — never the whole chain. mul doesn't know or care what came before a and b. It only stores "my rate with respect to my immediate children". The chain rule will stitch all these local links together later, automatically. This locality is what makes autograd simple enough to fit in a few lines.
Concrete example

Forward: let a = 3, b = 2, and c = a.mul(b). Then c.data = 6, c.children = [a, b], and c.localGrads = [2, 3] (that's [b, a]).

Backward from c: seed c.grad = 1, then push down: a.grad += localGrad × c.grad = 2 × 1 = 2, and b.grad += 3 × 1 = 3.

What a.grad = 2 means: nudge a from 3 up to 3.01, and the output c rises from 6 to 6.02 — a change of 0.02, i.e. 2× the nudge. The gradient is literally that multiplier, and it's what tells us how to adjust a to move the loss.

5. backward() — flowing gradients through the graph

The forward pass built a computation graph: every Value points back to its children. Now we run the chain rule backward across that whole graph in one sweep. Here is the method:

void backward() {
    List<Value> topo = new ArrayList<>();
    Set<Value> visited = new HashSet<>();
    buildTopo(this, topo, visited);     // 1) order the nodes
    grad[0] = 1;                        // 2) seed: d(loss)/d(loss) = 1
    for (int i = topo.size() - 1; i >= 0; i--) {  // 3) walk backward
        Value v = topo.get(i);
        for (int j = 0; j < v.children.length; j++)
            v.children[j].addGrad(
                v.localGrads[j] * v.grad[0]);  // chain rule!
    }
}
But why a backward() sweep — why not just compute each gradient directly? You could measure each parameter's gradient by brute force: nudge it, re-run the whole forward pass, and see how the loss moved. But that's one full forward pass per parameter — 4,192 of them, every single step. Backpropagation computes every gradient in a single backward sweep that costs about the same as one forward pass. That trick — all gradients for the price of one — is the entire reason neural networks are trainable in practice.

Three steps. Let's take them one at a time, then run a full example.

Step 1 — topological sort

Before we can propagate gradients backward, we need the nodes in a sensible order: every node must come after all the children it was built from. That ordering is a topological sort, built by buildTopo with a depth-first walk:

static void buildTopo(Value v, List<Value> topo, Set<Value> visited) {
    if (visited.add(v)) {              // skip if already seen
        for (Value c : v.children)
            buildTopo(c, topo, visited);  // children first
        topo.add(v);                   // then me
    }
}

The visited set guarantees each node is processed once, even if it feeds into many places. After this runs, topo lists leaves first and the final loss last. We then iterate it in reverse, so the loss is handled first and gradients flow toward the leaves.

But why bother with a topological sort? Because gradients can only flow in one order: a node's gradient isn't finished until every parent that uses it has pushed down its share. If d = c + 1 and c = a × b, we must finish d before we touch c — otherwise c would hand a half-computed gradient to a and b. Topological sort guarantees that "all parents before any child" ordering automatically, no matter how tangled the graph gets, so we never process a node too early.

Step 2 — seed the loss with gradient 1

We start the chain at the very end. The question "how much does the loss change if the loss changes?" answers itself: by exactly the same amount. So d(loss)/d(loss) = 1. That single line — grad[0] = 1 — kick-starts the entire backward flow.

Step 3 — the one line that is the chain rule

For each node, in reverse order, we push gradient down to its children:

child.grad  +=  localGrad × parent.grad

Read it as the chain rule made literal. parent.grad is "how much the loss cares about this parent" (the rate from here to the end). localGrad is "how much the parent cares about this child" (one local link). Multiply them and you get "how much the loss cares about the child". And it is +=, not =, because a child used in several places must add up all the gradient it receives from every parent.

But why does grad accumulate with += instead of =? Because one value can feed into several places. Say a is used in both c = a × b and d = a + e. Changing a then moves the loss through two separate paths, so a's true gradient is the sum of what flows back from c and from d — that's the multivariate chain rule. If we used =, the second path would overwrite the first and we'd silently lose half the gradient. += lets every parent add its own contribution.

Full worked example: loss = a × b + c

Take a = 3, b = 2, c = 4. The forward pass builds this graph (values flow left → right):

  a=3 ─┐
       ├─[ × ]── ab=6 ─┐
  b=2 ─┘               ├─[ + ]── loss=10
                 c=4 ──┘

Now backward (gradients flow right → left). Watch each child.grad += localGrad × parent.grad:

NodeWhat we doResult
lossSeed it.loss.grad = 1
+ nodeIt is the loss. Children ab and c, both local grads = 1. Push 1 × 1 to each.ab.grad += 1, c.grad += 1
× node (ab)Its grad is now 1. Children a, b with local grads b=2 and a=3. Push 2×1 and 3×1.a.grad += 2, b.grad += 3

Final gradients: a.grad = 2, b.grad = 3, c.grad = 1. Let's sanity-check a: if we bump a from 3 to 3.01, then ab goes from 6 to 6.02 (because b=2), and the loss rises by 0.02. That's a rate of 0.02 / 0.01 = 2 — exactly the gradient the engine computed. The math is real, and the engine got it right without us writing a single derivative.

7. Why does any of this matter?

microGPT has 4,192 parameters — 4,192 little numbers (the weights) that define the model's behavior. After one forward pass and one backward() call, every one of them has its .grad filled in. And each gradient is a precise instruction:

What a gradient tells you For each parameter: "If you increase me, the loss goes up by this much (positive grad) or down by this much (negative grad)." We want the loss low (the model less wrong). So we nudge every parameter a tiny step in the direction opposite its gradient. Loss goes down. Repeat a thousand times.
Real-life analogy A gradient is a compass. You're lost in foggy mountains and want to reach the valley (lowest loss). You can't see the valley, but you can feel which way the ground slopes under your feet — and that slope is the gradient. It tells you "take a step this way to go downhill." Gradient descent is just always stepping in the downhill direction, over and over, until you reach the bottom.

That repeated nudging — compute loss, call backward(), step every parameter against its gradient — is learning. There is no other magic. The Value class you just understood is the machinery that makes it possible, and the optimizer that does the nudging is the subject of the Training chapter.

Recap
  • A derivative is a rate of change: nudge the input, measure the output's response.
  • The chain rule multiplies local rates along a chain of operations.
  • A Value is a smart number storing data (forward) and grad (backward), plus its children and localGrads.
  • backward() topologically sorts the graph, seeds loss.grad = 1, and applies child.grad += localGrad × parent.grad in reverse.
  • Those gradients tell us how to change every parameter to lower the loss. That is learning.
