What is a GPT?

Beginner The big picture — no math, no code. Just the core idea, explained the way you'd explain it to a friend.

You've heard "GPT" a thousand times. ChatGPT, GPT-4, "a GPT model." But what is it, really? Strip away the hype and the marketing, and a GPT is something a programmer can fully understand. By the end of this page you'll know what the three letters mean, what the thing actually does, and why our 300-line Java program is the same idea as ChatGPT — just smaller.

The one-sentence version A GPT is a program that, given some text, guesses what comes next — and it learned how to guess by reading a huge amount of text and correcting itself millions of times.

Breaking down the name: G-P-T

"GPT" stands for Generative Pre-trained Transformer. Three words, three ideas. Let's take them one at a time, right to left actually makes the most sense — but we'll go in order.

G is for Generative

It generates — it produces new text, one piece at a time. It doesn't pick an answer from a fixed list, and it doesn't look things up in a database. It builds its output from scratch, character by character (in our tiny model) or word-fragment by word-fragment (in ChatGPT). "Generative" is the opposite of "retrieval." A search engine retrieves existing pages; a GPT generates something that didn't exist before.

P is for Pre-trained

Before you ever use it, the model has already been trained — it spent a long time studying examples and adjusting itself to get good at guessing. The "pre" means that learning happened ahead of time. When you run our Java program, it does this training first (takes a few minutes), and only then starts generating. ChatGPT was pre-trained once, for months, on thousands of machines — and now millions of people use that finished, frozen model.

T is for Transformer

"Transformer" is the name of the specific design inside — the particular arrangement of math that does the guessing. It was introduced in a 2017 research paper famously titled "Attention Is All You Need." You don't need to know how it works yet (that's chapters 6–8). For now: Transformer is just the brand of engine under the hood. There are other designs, but the Transformer is the one that won.

Mental shortcut Generative = makes new text · Pre-trained = already learned from examples · Transformer = the engine design that does it.

The autocomplete analogy

You already use a baby version of a GPT every day. When you type on your phone and it suggests the next word above the keyboard — that's the same fundamental idea. You type "I'll be there in five", and the phone offers "minutes."

A GPT is that, cranked up enormously. Your phone's autocomplete looks at the last word or two and offers a small handful of guesses. A GPT looks at everything you've written so far — a whole paragraph, a whole conversation, an entire document — and produces a far more sophisticated guess about what should come next. Same job. Vastly more capable.

Real-life analogy Next-token prediction is just autocomplete on your phone. When you type "See you tom", your phone suggests "tomorrow" — it learned from millions of texts that "tomorrow" usually follows "tom" in that context. A GPT does exactly the same thing, only much better and for every possible context, not just the last word or two.
Why "just guessing the next word" is secretly powerful To correctly guess the next word in "The capital of France is ___", you have to know the capital of France. To finish "2 + 2 = ___" you have to do arithmetic. To continue a story sensibly you have to track characters and plot. "Predict the next token" sounds trivial, but doing it well forces the model to absorb a huge amount about the world. That's the trick the whole field is built on.

"Next token prediction" — the actual job

The technical name for what a GPT does is next token prediction. Let's make that concrete. Suppose the model has read:

"The cat sat on the ?"

A trained model doesn't return a single answer. It returns a ranked list of possibilities with confidence scores, something like:

Next word guessModel's confidence
mat61%
floor14%
roof9%
chair5%
...everything else...11%

Then it picks one (usually a likely one, but with a dash of randomness so it's not robotic), appends it to the text, and repeats the whole process to get the word after that. Guess, append, guess, append. That loop — run over and over — is how a GPT writes a paragraph, an email, or a name.

Notice the loop The model only ever predicts one step ahead. Long, coherent text comes from doing that one small step thousands of times, each time feeding its own last guess back in as part of the input.
Concrete example

Input: the three letters emm (the model has read this far into a name).

Output: a probability for each of the 27 tokens, e.g. {a: 0.72, y: 0.08, e: 0.05, i: 0.04, …, BOS: 0.01}

Meaning: the model is 72% confident the next character is a — because emma is by far the most common name in the data that starts with emm. Pick a, append it, and the input becomes emma; predict again and BOS (end-of-name) now wins, so the name stops.

But why predict the next token? Why not the whole word or sentence at once? Because predicting one small piece at a time is far simpler — there's only one decision to make, over a tiny set of choices (27 for us). Predicting a whole word or sentence in one shot would mean choosing from billions of possibilities at once. And you lose nothing: since you feed each guess back in and predict again, chaining these one-step predictions lets you generate text of any length. One simple, repeatable step beats one giant, impossible one.

What is a "token"?

I've been saying "word," but that's a simplification. The real unit a GPT works with is called a token. A token is just "one chunk of text" — the smallest piece the model reads and writes at a time. What counts as a chunk is a design choice.

  • In ChatGPT, a token is roughly a word-fragment ("transform" + "er", or "ing").
  • In our tiny Java GPT, a token is a single character. One letter at a time.

Why characters? Because our model learns to generate human names, and names are short. Working one letter at a time keeps everything small and understandable. The complete set of tokens the model knows is called its vocabulary. Our vocabulary is tiny: the 26 lowercase letters, plus one special token.

But why characters instead of words? It's a tradeoff. Real GPTs use "subwords" — pieces of words — which makes them faster (fewer steps per sentence) and lets them carry meaning in each token. Characters are the simpler choice: the vocabulary stays tiny (27 instead of 100,000+), and you never have to decide where to split a word. The cost is that the model has to learn spelling completely from scratch, letter by letter. For a toy that generates short names, that's a fine price to pay for simplicity you can see.
abcdef ghijkl mnopqr stuvwx yzBOS

That's 27 tokens total. The odd one out is BOS, which stands for Beginning Of Sequence. It's a marker the model uses to say "a name starts here" and "a name ends here" — like a bookend. It's not a letter you'd ever print; it's punctuation for the machine. When the model generates a BOS, that's its way of saying "the name is finished."

Key takeaway The model never sees letters. Internally, each token is just a number: a→0, b→1, … z→25, BOS→26. Everything a GPT does is arithmetic on numbers. We'll spend the next two chapters on exactly how text becomes numbers.

What is "the model," physically?

Here's the part that surprises most programmers. You might imagine the model is a clever algorithm — pages of branching logic, rules, if-statements. It isn't. The model is just a big pile of numbers.

These numbers are called parameters (you'll also hear "weights"). They start out completely random — pure noise. Training is the process of nudging every one of those numbers, slightly, again and again, until the pile as a whole produces good guesses. The "intelligence" of the model lives entirely in the specific values of these numbers. Change the numbers, change the behavior. That's the whole machine.

How big is our pile? Exactly 4,192 numbers. That's it. A spreadsheet could hold them. For comparison, GPT-4 has roughly 1.8 trillion — about 400 million times more. But — and this is the entire point of this course — the recipe is identical. Same kind of numbers, same kind of arithmetic, same training idea. Only the scale differs.

But why only 4,192 parameters? Is that enough to learn anything? It's roughly the smallest pile that can still learn something useful — small enough to fit in a spreadsheet, yet enough to capture the patterns of short names. So why does GPT-4 have ~1.8 trillion? Because more parameters means more room to store patterns: more spellings, more grammar, more facts, more of the world. Our 4,192 numbers can learn "names tend to end in vowels"; you need billions to learn "the capital of France is Paris." The algorithm is identical at both sizes — capacity is the only thing that grows.

The training loop, in plain English

So how does a pile of random numbers become a pile of good numbers? Through a loop. Here is the entire learning process with no math whatsoever:

  1. Show it a real example. Take a real name from the dataset, say emma, and reveal it one letter at a time.
  2. Let it guess. After the letters em, ask the model: what's the next letter? It produces its ranked guesses.
  3. Check the answer. We know the real next letter is m. How much confidence did the model put on m? If it was confident, good. If it guessed z, bad.
  4. Measure the wrongness. Turn "how bad was that guess" into a single number called the loss. High loss = very wrong. Low loss = nearly right.
  5. Nudge the numbers. Adjust all 4,192 parameters a tiny bit in the direction that would have made the correct letter more likely next time. (The mechanism for figuring out which direction is the magic of chapters 5 and 9 — for now, trust that it can.)
  6. Repeat — a thousand times. New example, guess, check, nudge. Over and over. Each nudge is microscopic, but a thousand of them add up to a model that has genuinely learned the patterns of names.
This is the whole secret There is no hand-written rule like "names often end in a vowel." Nobody programs that in. The model discovers such patterns on its own, purely by being corrected toward real examples thousands of times. Learning = repeated correction. That's it. That's what "training" means.

Watching it actually learn

This isn't hypothetical — it's our real Java program. When you run it, the first thing you see is the loss going down as training proceeds. A falling loss means "the model's guesses are getting less wrong." Here's abbreviated real output:

num docs: 32033 vocab size: 27 num params: 4192 step 1/1000 | loss 3.4011 ← random guessing, very wrong step 100/1000 | loss 2.6420 step 300/1000 | loss 2.4115 step 600/1000 | loss 2.2837 step 1000/1000 | loss 2.1903 ← much better

Notice the loss starts around 3.4 and drops to about 2.2. That fall is the learning. At step 1, the numbers are random and the model is basically guessing letters blindly. By step 1000, it has been nudged a thousand times and its guesses now reflect the real shape of human names.

But why does the loss actually go down? Because every single training step adjusts those 4,192 numbers in the exact direction that makes the correct next letter a little more likely. Loss is just "how wrong was the guess," so making the right answer more likely makes the loss smaller — by construction. Do that a thousand times and the tiny improvements compound: the model slides from random guessing (loss ~3.4) to having genuinely absorbed the patterns in English names (loss ~2.2). A falling loss isn't magic; it's the visible trace of a thousand small corrections.
Concrete example

What the loss number actually means, step by step:

Step 1 — loss ≈ 3.30: pure random guessing. With 27 equally-likely tokens the loss is −log(1/27) ≈ 3.30. (Our real run starts a touch higher, ~3.4, because the random weights aren't even perfectly even.)

Step ~300 — loss ≈ 2.4: better than random. The model has learned that some letters are simply more common than others — lots of names use a, e, n; almost none use q.

Step 1000 — loss ≈ 2.2: it has absorbed real letter-to-letter patterns — which letters tend to follow which, where names like to end. Each 0.1 drop in loss is the model becoming measurably less surprised by real names.

Then comes the fun part. With training done, we ask the model to generate — start from BOS and keep guessing the next letter until it produces a BOS to end the name. These are brand-new names the model invented; none of them are copied from the training data:

--- inference (new, hallucinated names) --- sample 1: shade sample 2: jara sample 3: amara sample 4: kayven sample 5: marlon ...

Look at those. shade, jara, amara — they feel like names. They're pronounceable, they have name-like rhythm, they end in plausible ways. The model was never told a single rule about how names work. It absorbed all of it from being corrected toward 32,000 examples. A pile of 4,192 numbers, nudged into shape, now dreams up names.

The same thing ChatGPT does When ChatGPT writes you a paragraph, it's running this identical loop at inference time: look at everything so far, guess the next token, append it, repeat. The only differences are scale (trillions of numbers instead of thousands) and vocabulary (word-fragments instead of letters). If you understand the name generator, you understand the shape of the whole thing.

Where we go from here

We've made two big promises that we haven't paid off yet, and the rest of the course pays them off:

  • "Text becomes numbers." How exactly? That's vectors (Chapter 2) and embeddings (Chapter 3).
  • "We nudge the numbers in the right direction." How does the program know which direction? That's gradients and automatic differentiation (Chapters 4–5, 9).

Everything starts with the most basic building block of all — a list of numbers called a vector. That's next, and it comes with demos you can drag around.

Before you move on Make sure these four ideas feel solid: (1) a GPT generates text by predicting one token at a time; (2) a token is one chunk of text — a character, for us; (3) the model is just a pile of numbers (parameters); (4) training means correcting those numbers toward real examples, over and over. If those click, you're ready for the math — and it'll be gentler than you expect.
