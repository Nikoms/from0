Vectors & Linear Algebra

Beginner The single most important idea in this whole course — and it's simpler than its scary name. This page is hands-on: drag things, watch numbers change.

"Linear algebra" sounds like a course you'd avoid in college. Forget the name. The only thing you actually need is one humble idea: a list of numbers. That's a vector. Everything else on this page is just things you can do with lists of numbers, and we'll build them up one small step at a time, with pictures you can manipulate.

What is a vector? A vector is just an ordered list of numbers. [72] is a vector. [3, 5] is a vector. [1.2, -0.4, 8.0, 3.3, 0.1] is a vector. As a Java programmer you already know this type — it's a double[]. You've been using vectors for years without the fancy word.
Concrete example

The same idea at different sizes — each is just a list of numbers:

1D — a temperature: [72] → one number on a thermometer.

2D — a GPS coordinate: [37.77, −122.42] → San Francisco.

3D — an RGB color: [255, 128, 0] → orange.

16D — our GPT's embedding of the letter 'a': [0.03, −0.12, 0.07, 0.15, −0.04, 0.09, −0.11, 0.02, 0.06, −0.08, 0.14, −0.01, 0.05, −0.13, 0.10, −0.03] → we can't picture it, but addition, dot products, and distance work on it exactly as they do on [72].

Real-life analogy A vector is a recipe. A chocolate cake might be [200g flour, 150g sugar, 100g butter, 50g cocoa] — that's a 4-dimensional vector! Two recipes with similar ingredient vectors taste similar. And you can subtract recipes to find the difference: [+50g cocoa, −30g sugar] = "more chocolatey, less sweet." The arithmetic on the list of numbers is the arithmetic on the thing itself.

Why does AI need vectors at all?

Recall from Chapter 1: a GPT is a pile of numbers doing arithmetic. But the things we care about — letters, words, names — aren't numbers. A computer can't multiply the letter k by anything. So before any AI math can happen, we must turn everything into numbers.

And not just one number — usually a list of numbers, because one number can't capture much. To describe a person you need several facts (height, age, …). To describe the "meaning" of a word, a GPT uses a list of 16 numbers (in our model) or 12,000 numbers (in GPT-4). That list is a vector. Vectors are the language every part of a neural network speaks. Master this page and the rest of the course is downhill.

But why do we need vectors at all? Why not a single number per word? Because computers only understand numbers, and a single number can't capture the richness of a letter or a word. Think of GPS: two numbers (latitude, longitude) pin down a location far better than one street number ever could — the second number adds a whole new axis of information. A vector is the same idea taken further: a list of numbers lets you encode many properties of a thing at once, so the model has room to express "this character is vowel-ish, name-start-ish, rarely-doubled" all in one bundle.

1D: a vector with one number

Let's start at the absolute bottom. A vector with a single number, [x], is called a 1-dimensional (1D) vector. "Dimensions" just means "how many numbers are in the list." One number, one dimension.

A single number is naturally a position on a line — a thermometer, a volume knob, a slider. The temperature 72°F is the 1D vector [72]: one number, one spot on the number line. Drag the slider and watch the dot move. That dot is the vector.

2D: a vector with two numbers

Add a second number and you get a 2D vector, [x, y]. Two numbers describe a point on a flat surface — exactly like coordinates on a map or pixels on your screen. The first number is how far right, the second is how far up.

There are two equally correct ways to picture a 2D vector, and both matter later:

  • As a point — a single dot at position (x, y).
  • As an arrow — pointing from the origin (0,0) out to that point. The arrow shows both a direction and a length.

In the demo below, drag the head of either arrow. Arrow A and arrow B are both yours to move. As you drag, watch every number on the right update live. We'll explain each of those numbers right after.

Vector addition — just add the lists

To add two vectors you add them slot by slot: [3, 1] + [1, 2] = [4, 3]. Geometrically this is the parallelogram rule: lay arrow B's tail at A's head, and the sum is the arrow from the origin to where you end up. Toggle "show parallelogram" in the demo and drag — you'll see A and B form two sides of a parallelogram, and the purple sum is its diagonal. Addition is "do one move, then the other."

[a₁, a₂] + [b₁, b₂] = [a₁+b₁,   a₂+b₂]

The dot product — "do these point the same way?"

The dot product is the operation you'll meet most in this whole course (attention, in Chapter 7, is built almost entirely out of dot products). It takes two vectors and returns a single number. The recipe: multiply matching slots, then add up the results.

A · B = a₁×b₁ + a₂×b₂

So [3, 1] · [1, 2] = 3×1 + 1×2 = 5. But the number itself is less important than what its sign tells you:

  • Large positive dot product → the two arrows point in similar directions.
  • Near zero → the arrows are roughly perpendicular (at a right angle) — unrelated.
  • Negative → the arrows point in opposite directions.
Try this In Demo 2, drag B so it points the same way as A — the dot product gets big and positive. Now drag B to a right angle (90°) against A — watch the dot product pass through zero. Keep going until B points opposite A — it goes negative. The dot product is a "same-direction meter." That single fact is the heart of how a GPT decides which words are relevant to each other.
But why does the dot product measure similarity? Look at the recipe: you multiply matching slots and add them up. When two arrows point the same way, their slots have the same signs, so every product is positive and they pile up into a big positive total. When the arrows are perpendicular, the positive and negative products cancel out and you land near zero. When they point opposite ways, the products are mostly negative and the total goes negative. So the dot product is really answering one question: "how much do these two arrows agree?" That's exactly what "similar" means in number-space.

Length and distance — Pythagoras returns

The length (or "magnitude") of a vector is how long its arrow is. It's just the Pythagorean theorem you half-remember from school: |A| = √(a₁² + a₂²). The distance between two vectors is the length of the gap between their heads — same formula, applied to the difference:

distance(A, B) = √((a₁−b₁)² + (a₂−b₂)²)

Why care? Because once words are vectors (Chapter 3), distance becomes meaning. Two words whose vectors sit close together are treated as similar; far apart means unrelated. "Close in number-space = similar in meaning" is the idea the entire field rests on.

Key takeaway Three operations, three intuitions, and you've got the core toolkit: addition = combine/move, dot product = how-much-same-direction, distance = how-different. Everything a neural network computes is built from these.

3D: add a third number

A 3D vector [x, y, z] is a point in space — like the corner of a room measured as (across, up, depth). Same idea as 2D, just one more number. The math doesn't change at all: addition is still slot-by-slot, the dot product still multiplies-and-sums all three slots, distance still uses Pythagoras (now with three terms).

The only new difficulty is drawing it — your screen is flat, but the vector lives in 3D. We solve that by projecting: squashing 3D onto the 2D screen, the way a photograph flattens the real world. Drag inside the demo to rotate the view and convince yourself it's a genuine 3D arrow. Use the sliders to change the vector's three numbers.

5D and beyond: where pictures give up

Here's the leap that trips people up, so let's say it plainly: a 5D vector is just a list of five numbers. Nothing more mysterious than a 2D vector — it has three extra slots. We cannot draw a 5D arrow, because we have no 5-directional space to draw in. But the math is completely unchanged.

But why can't we just visualize 5D? It's not a lack of cleverness — it's biology. Our brains evolved to navigate the 3D world we live in, so we simply have no neural hardware for picturing a fourth or fifth perpendicular direction. Try it and your mind quietly reuses the three axes it already has. The reassuring part: the math doesn't share this limitation. Addition, dot product, and distance are just arithmetic on lists of numbers, and they work identically whether the list has 2 entries or 12,000. You don't have to see it for it to be true.

A concrete 5D example. Describe a person with five facts:

person = [height_cm, weight_kg, age, income_k, happiness]
Alice  = [165, 60, 30, 55, 8]
Bob    = [180, 85, 45, 90, 6]

Want to know how similar Alice and Bob are? Use the exact same distance formula as the 2D demo, just with five terms under the square root. The dot product? Same — multiply all five matching slots, add them up. Every tool you learned on the 2D plane works, unchanged, in 5 or 16 or 12,000 dimensions. That is the quiet superpower of vectors: the operations don't care how many dimensions there are.

Key takeaway Stop trying to visualize high dimensions — nobody can, not even researchers. Instead, trust the arithmetic. When this course says a token becomes a "16-dimensional vector," your mental image should simply be: double[16], a list of 16 numbers, on which we run addition, dot products, and distances exactly as you saw in 2D.

Seeing the unseeable: dimensionality reduction

There's a real problem, though. If a word's meaning is a 16-number vector, and we want to check whether the model grouped similar words together, we can't look at 16D. So we cheat — cleverly. We project the high-dimensional data down to 2D so it fits on a screen, trying hard to keep the important structure intact. This is called dimensionality reduction.

But why bother reducing dimensions at all? Purely because we're human and we need to see patterns to trust them. The model is perfectly happy working in 16 dimensions — it never looks at a picture. But we want to check things like "did vowels end up clustered together?", and our eyes only do 2D. PCA finds the two directions in the high-dimensional cloud where the data spreads out the most, then flattens everything onto those two axes. It's lossy — you throw away the other dimensions — but what survives is usually enough to reveal the clusters. It's a tool for us, not for the model.
The shadow analogy Hold your hand up to a lamp and it casts a 2D shadow on the wall. The shadow throws away depth, yet you can still recognize the hand's shape — fingers, spread, gaps. Dimensionality reduction is casting a smart shadow of high-dimensional data onto a 2D wall, chosen at the angle that preserves the most shape.

PCA — choosing the best angle for the shadow

The most common method is PCA (Principal Component Analysis). Don't let the name intimidate you. PCA answers one question: "Of all the angles I could shine the light from, which one casts the shadow that spreads the data out the most?" The angle along which the data varies most is called the first principal component; the next-best angle (at a right angle to the first) is the second. Use those two as your 2D axes, and you get the most informative flat picture possible.

What you now know

  • A vector is a list of numbers — a double[].
  • Dimensions = how many numbers are in the list.
  • Addition combines vectors slot-by-slot (the parallelogram).
  • The dot product is a "same-direction meter" — one number, sign tells you similar / perpendicular / opposite.
  • Distance measures how different two vectors are (Pythagoras).
  • These operations work identically in any number of dimensions, even ones you can't picture.
  • PCA projects high-D data to 2D so we can see its structure — a smart shadow.
